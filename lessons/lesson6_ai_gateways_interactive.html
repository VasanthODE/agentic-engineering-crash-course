AI gateways

Your agent talks directly to one model from one provider. In production that's fragile and expensive. A gateway is a single control point between your app and the providers — and because every request flows through it, you control everything from one place.

01 Why a gateway

The single control point

Talking directly to providers means all the operational logic — routing, retries, cost, rate limits, keys — lives tangled inside your app. A gateway pulls it into one place every request flows through.

Without a gateway
With a gateway
The chokepoint is the leverage. A gateway isn't a model and isn't smart — its power comes purely from its POSITION in the request path. Same pattern as an API gateway or load balancer, specialized for the cost and failure modes of LLM traffic.
02 Highest-value function

Model routing

"Always use the most powerful model" is wrong on both axes — cost and quality. Route each request to the right-sized model: a smaller one is often faster and good enough, and ~18× cheaper.

Pick the right-sized model, not the best one. Decide by complexity (rule-based like your production agent's ModelRouter, or a cheap classifier model), and also by capability, availability, and policy. Across millions of calls, routing is the biggest lever on the bill.
03 Survivability

Fallbacks & reliability

Routing picks the best model — useless if it's down. The gateway makes a flaky dependency look reliable: it catches failures, retries with backoff, and fails over to another provider, all invisible to your app.

Retry for transient, fall back for persistent — and fail fast on a bad request. Backoff prevents a thundering herd; a circuit breaker stops re-probing a dead provider. You accept degraded-but-available over failed.
04 The managed bill

Cost control

You pay per token, and output costs far more than input. The chokepoint is the one place that sees every token — so it's where you make spend visible, attributed, bounded, and reusable.

05 Throughput

Rate limiting & load

Providers cap how fast you can call them, but real traffic is bursty. A raw burst gets a wall of 429s. The gateway sits between and shapes the traffic to fit the limit.

Trade a little latency for zero dropped traffic. Token-bucket shaping, queuing with backpressure and priority, load-balancing across keys/providers (proactive 429-avoidance), and inbound per-user limits that protect you from your own users.
One layer, one idea: the chokepoint is the leverage. Routing sends each request to the right-sized model · fallbacks absorb provider failure with retries, backoff, and circuit breakers · cost control makes spend visible, attributed, and capped · rate limiting shapes bursty traffic in both directions. A gateway isn't intelligent — its power is its position. The infrastructure around the agent's brain.
