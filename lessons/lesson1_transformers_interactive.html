AI Agent Engineering · Lesson 1

How a transformer actually works

One model, one job: predict the next word. Everything else — attention, knowledge, reasoning — is the machinery that computes those odds. Here it is, end to end, one concept forcing the next.

01 First principle

It only ever predicts the next word

A transformer is a function that maps a sequence of tokens to a probability distribution over the next token — roughly 100,000 numbers that sum to 1. Answering questions, writing code, calling tools: all of it is this one task, scaled.

The spread has a name: entropy. A 92% spike (the capital of France) is low entropy — the model is certain. Six candidates near 15% is high entropy — many valid continuations. That single idea is why temperature exists.
02 Words become numbers

Embeddings: meaning as coordinates

The model can't read words — it reads numbers. But arbitrary IDs (cat=1, dog=2) invent false relationships. The fix: give every word hundreds of coordinates in a space where distance means meaning.

This map is a useful lie. Real embeddings aren't 2D — Claude's are ~4,096-dimensional, with thousands of independent directions for tense, gender, sentiment, topic. The clusters self-organised during training, purely from co-occurrence.
03 Words look at each other

Attention: context edits meaning

Embeddings are static — "cat" gets the same vector everywhere. But "cat" near "hurricane" should differ from "cat" near "sat." Attention lets each word look around and update itself based on its neighbours.

Click a word to make it the one "looking around." Thicker lines = more attention.
Same word, different neighbours — watch the meaning shift:
"The cat sat on the mat" "The cat 5 storm hit" "The cat ate because it was hungry"
The payoff: in sentence 1, "cat" attends to "sat"/"mat" (animal). In sentence 2, the identical starting vector attends to "5"/"storm" (hurricane). Attention is what made them diverge.

05 A team of specialists

Multi-head: many relationships at once

One head learns one pattern. So transformers run dozens of heads in parallel, each with its own Q/K/V, each specialising in a different relationship — all on the same sentence.

Same sentence, different head. Click a head to see what it tracks:
Claude runs ~96 heads per layer. No head was told its job — each discovered its specialty during training because that division of labour minimised prediction error. Their outputs merge through one matrix, W_O.
06 The thinking step

Feed-forward: where facts live

If attention is how words talk, the FFN is how each word thinks — alone. And this unglamorous two-layer network is where the model stores most of what it actually knows.

07 The backbone

The residual stream

Stack 80 layers naively and the signal smears into noise. The fix is one symbol: +. Each layer adds a correction instead of replacing — preserving the original all the way up.

08 How it learned everything

Backpropagation: the one loop

Nothing you've seen was designed — it was learned. One loop built all of it: predict, measure surprise, flow the error back, nudge every weight. Repeat billions of times.

The learning loop Gradient flow The hard step
Step through it on "The cat sat on the ___" where the true word is "mat":
The error flows backward — the reverse of the forward pass. Press play:
▶ Flow the gradient backward
Forward (purple, →) computes the prediction. Backward (coral, ←) computes how every weight should change. That's backpropagation: the chain rule through every layer.
Why the softmax gradient is the trickiest part of training:
Because attention weights must sum to 1.0, they're all coupled — nudge one up, the others drop. So each weight's gradient depends on all the others: a full n×n matrix (the Jacobian), not n independent numbers. The densest step in the backward pass — handled automatically by frameworks.
This loop is the whole miracle. Run "predict, measure surprise, nudge" on a fraction of everything humans have written, and structure nobody programmed emerges: specialist heads, stored facts, self-organised meaning.
